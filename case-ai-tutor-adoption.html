Case Study

AI Tutor Adoption

Personalization and access vs. privacy, surveillance, and human support.

Start caseOpen worksheet

Best For

  • Educational technology
  • Foundations of education
  • Methods courses
  • Field-experience seminars

Scenario

A district wants 24/7 tutoring.

A school district is considering an AI tutoring platform for grades 6-12. The tool promises personalized practice, instant feedback, multilingual support, and dashboards that help teachers see where students are struggling.

To personalize support, the tutor collects student logins, assignment responses, time on task, hints requested, error patterns, and chat transcripts. The vendor says the system can help students who cannot access after-school tutoring, but some teachers worry that students may be monitored too closely or routed away from human support.

Decision Point

Should the district adopt the AI tutor for student use? If yes, under what conditions?

Stakeholders

Who should be considered?

Students

May gain support, but may also give up learning data, privacy, or access to human help.

Teachers

May gain insight and efficiency, but may need to interpret dashboards and correct AI mistakes.

Families

Need to understand what data is collected and what alternatives exist.

District Leaders

Must weigh access, cost, procurement, equity, and accountability.

Four-Question Analysis

Use the framework.

Who benefits?

Students needing extra practice, teachers seeking patterns, families needing flexible support, and vendors selling the platform.

Who risks harm?

Students whose data is misread, students needing human support, students with disabilities, multilingual learners, and heavily monitored students.

Who pays the cost?

Students may pay with data and reduced privacy; teachers may pay with added monitoring work; districts pay financially and reputationally.

Who gets to decide?

Procurement teams may decide before students, families, and teachers have meaningful input.

Discussion Prompts

Questions for class discussion

  1. What would make this tool genuinely supportive rather than merely efficient?
  2. What student data is necessary for tutoring, and what data is excessive?
  3. When should a student be routed to a human teacher, tutor, counselor, or specialist?
  4. What would families need to know before student use begins?

Possible Safeguards

  • Teacher-reviewed recommendations
  • Data minimization
  • Family notification and opt-out
  • Human support escalation
  • Accessibility review
