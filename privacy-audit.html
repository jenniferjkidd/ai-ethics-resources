AI Tool Privacy Audit

Use this audit before asking teachers or students to create accounts, upload files, paste student work, enter personal information, or use an AI tool for classroom tasks.

Start audit

Bottom Line

If you cannot explain what data goes in, where it may go, and who can access it, pause before using the tool with students.

  • What data is collected?
  • Can prompts or uploads train future models?
  • Who can access student information?
  • Can students opt out?

Purpose

Make privacy visible before the tool enters the classroom.

AI tools can make teaching tasks easier, but they can also collect prompts, uploaded files, student work, usage data, images, voice, location, or account information. This audit helps preservice teachers ask practical privacy questions before adoption.

Step 1

Identify the tool and intended use.

Privacy decisions depend on context. The same tool may be lower risk for teacher planning and higher risk for student-facing use.

Step 2

Map the data.

List what people would need to enter, upload, connect, or reveal in order to use the tool.

Student identity

Names, emails, usernames, student IDs, photos, voice, demographics, disability status, or location.

Student work

Writing, assessments, discussion posts, projects, images, recordings, reflections, or feedback history.

Learning or behavior data

Performance patterns, engagement, mistakes, intervention flags, attendance, browsing, or classroom behavior.

Teacher or school data

Lesson materials, classroom rosters, institutional documents, curriculum, rubrics, or internal communications.

Step 3

Read for the questions that matter.

Tool policies can be long and unclear. Focus first on these classroom-relevant questions.

Collection

  • What data does the tool collect from prompts, uploads, accounts, or usage?
  • Does it collect more than is needed for the classroom purpose?

Storage and retention

  • How long is data kept?
  • Can the teacher, student, or school delete it?

Training and reuse

  • Can prompts, uploads, outputs, or feedback be used to train or improve models?
  • Is there a clear opt-out?

Access and sharing

  • Who can access the data: the company, vendors, school administrators, other users, or third parties?
  • Is data shared for advertising, analytics, research, or product development?

Step 4

Check consent, transparency, and alternatives.

Ethical privacy decisions are not only technical. They also involve whether students and families know what is happening and have meaningful choices.

Step 5

Make a privacy recommendation.

The goal is not to memorize every privacy policy. The goal is to decide whether the tool fits the educational purpose without asking students to give up more data, privacy, or control than the learning goal justifies.
