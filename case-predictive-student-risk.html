Case Study

Predictive Student-Risk System

Early intervention vs. labeling, bias, and unequal monitoring.

Start caseOpen worksheet

Best For

  • School policy
  • Equity and access
  • Data literacy
  • Student support systems

Scenario

A dashboard predicts which students need intervention.

A district wants to use a predictive system that flags students who may be at risk academically or behaviorally. It combines attendance, grades, behavior reports, platform logins, assignment completion, and prior intervention records.

Support staff hope the system will help them intervene earlier. Teachers worry that students may be labeled based on incomplete data, and families wonder whether the system will follow students across schools or affect opportunities.

Decision Point

Should the district use predictive risk flags? What limits and review processes are required?

Stakeholders

Who should be considered?

Students

May receive needed support or be stigmatized by labels.

Teachers

May gain information but may also inherit biased or incomplete risk labels.

Support Staff

Need useful information without replacing relational knowledge.

Families

Need transparency, appeal, and clarity about consequences.

Four-Question Analysis

Use the framework.

Who benefits?

Students needing early support, counselors managing caseloads, administrators tracking interventions, and vendors providing analytics.

Who risks harm?

Students with biased records, students experiencing poverty or instability, students with disabilities, and students whose behavior is misunderstood.

Who pays the cost?

Students pay if labels reduce trust or opportunity; teachers pay if they must correct system errors; families pay if they must challenge opaque decisions.

Who gets to decide?

District leaders may adopt the system before students, families, and teachers understand how risk scores are created or used.

Discussion Prompts

Questions for class discussion

  1. What should a risk flag be allowed to trigger?
  2. What should it never be allowed to trigger?
  3. How can students and families challenge inaccurate or harmful labels?
  4. How should human knowledge and student voice be included?

Possible Safeguards

  • No automated high-stakes decisions
  • Human review required
  • Family transparency
  • Appeal and correction process
  • Regular equity audits
