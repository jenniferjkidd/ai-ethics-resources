Data Privacy and Surveillance

Welcome to the fishbowl: a lesson on how AI systems collect, connect, analyze, and act on data in ways that affect privacy, autonomy, and power.

Start lesson Open privacy audit

Learning Targets

  • Explain how AI systems collect and analyze personal data.
  • Assess strategies to protect data and privacy in a connected world.

Teacher Education Frame

Why this matters for preservice teachers

Teachers make daily decisions about apps, platforms, learning tools, family communication, student work, and classroom data. This lesson helps preservice teachers see privacy not only as a personal habit, but as a professional responsibility tied to student dignity, autonomy, and consent.

Use This Lesson As

A student-facing lesson, discussion anchor, privacy audit warm-up, or bridge into classroom AI tool evaluation.

Opening

Welcome to the fishbowl

Remember when privacy meant closing your door?

In the AI age, your door might be collecting data on how often you open it, what time you usually get home, and whether you are more of a “slam it shut” or “gentle close” person.

Welcome to the wild world of data privacy and surveillance.

Digital Breadcrumbs

What is actually happening to your data?

Every time you interact with technology, you leave behind “data exhaust.” Think of it like a digital breadcrumb trail.

2 AM shopping

Recorded, analyzed, and probably used to show you ads for the next three weeks.

Fitness app routes

A company may know where you live, when you are not home, and how your routines change.

Social quizzes

Personality quizzes can collect surprisingly detailed psychological signals.

Classroom tools

Student work, logins, errors, time-on-task, and feedback histories can become data trails too.

Interactive Checkpoint

Take 30 seconds. Open your phone settings and check which apps have access to your location. Surprised? Horrified? Both are valid reactions.

The AI Twist

Why this is not your parents’ privacy problem

In the old days, companies collected data, but it mostly sat there. Now AI can connect, infer, predict, and act on data in ways that may not be visible to the person being analyzed.

1. AI can connect dots you did not know existed

Imagine you search for “constantly tired,” order more coffee than usual, skip your usual jogs, and browse job listings at odd hours.

An AI system could infer that you are unhappy at work, predict you might quit, or guess something about your health without you explicitly telling anyone.

2. AI can predict your future, sort of

AI systems use past behavior to predict future actions: whether someone may repay a loan, get an interview, need intervention, or see a different price.

Think about it: If an AI predicted something negative about your future based on your data, would you even know? Would you have a chance to prove it wrong?

Examples

Real-world examples that raise privacy questions

These examples show how data collection can cross from convenience into inference, surveillance, and social control.

Example 1: Shopping patterns and pregnancy prediction

A widely discussed example describes how shopping patterns were used to infer that a teenager was pregnant before her family knew. The larger issue is not one store; it is the power of everyday data to reveal sensitive life information.

Original New York Times article

Example 2: Doorbell cameras and neighborhood surveillance

Doorbell cameras can create neighborhood-level surveillance networks. The ethical question is who is recorded, who controls the footage, and who can access it.

EFF discussion of Ring and police access

Example 3: Your face as your boarding pass

Some airports use facial recognition for travel processes. It may be convenient, but it also raises questions about biometric databases, travel patterns, consent, and future uses.

Surveillance Gradient

It is not all or nothing

Not all data collection is equally invasive. Think of it as a spectrum.

Low Stakes

Netflix knowing you watched a cooking show and recommending another show.

Medium Stakes

A health app tracking your steps, especially if others want access to that data.

High Stakes

Location tracking that could reveal health, religion, relationships, routines, or political activity.

Very High Stakes

Facial recognition combined with AI-enabled tracking across public spaces.

Activity Idea

Create a “data privacy score” for different technologies you engaged with over the past week. Rate each on a 1-10 scale for creepiness, sensitivity, or potential harm.

Balance

The upside of smart data

Data collection is not always the villain of the story. When handled thoughtfully, AI and data analytics can make life easier, safer, and more inclusive.

Personalization

Recommendations, playlists, and routing can save time and effort.

Health and Safety

Fitness trackers, traffic sensors, and emergency systems can identify patterns and respond faster.

Accessibility

Captioning, text-to-speech, and translation tools rely on large datasets to support access.

Public Good

Aggregated data can help researchers and responders understand patterns during crises.

The challenge is not simply that data is used. The challenge is how it is used, who controls it, who benefits, who is harmed, and whether people have meaningful choices.

Why Care?

Besides the obvious creep factor

The discrimination problem

AI systems trained on biased or proxy data can affect decisions connected to loans, jobs, housing, sentencing, school support, or discipline.

  • Zip code may correlate with race and income.
  • Social media connections may be used for profiling.
  • Shopping and browsing patterns can feed predictions.
The “nothing to hide” myth

Privacy is not about hiding bad things. It is about controlling your own information and limiting how others can use it.

  • Would you want an employer seeing mental health app data?
  • Would you want insurance companies tracking food purchases?
  • Would you want political, religious, or relationship data inferred and categorized?
The data breach domino effect

Data in databases can become a target. When breaches happen, information can be sold, reused, combined with other leaks, or used for identity theft.

Action

What can you actually do?

You cannot completely opt out of modern life, but you can make more conscious choices.

Easy Wins

  • Review app permissions regularly.
  • Turn off ad personalization when possible.
  • Use privacy-focused browsers or extensions.
  • Skim privacy settings and summaries before using a tool.

Medium Effort

  • Use two-factor authentication.
  • Delete old accounts you no longer use.
  • Separate accounts or emails for different purposes.
  • Be cautious on public Wi-Fi.

Advanced Mode

  • Use encrypted messaging when appropriate.
  • Explore alternatives to major data-driven platforms.
  • Set automatic data deletion where available.
  • Audit what data is connected across accounts.

Challenge

Pick one thing from the “Easy Wins” list and do it now.

Bigger Picture

Surveillance is not only individual

This is not just about personal settings. Widespread surveillance plus AI creates social and political effects.

Chilling Effects

When people know they are being watched, they may self-censor or behave differently.

Power Imbalances

Companies and governments may know far more about people than people know about them.

Social Sorting

AI can categorize people and treat them differently based on predicted characteristics.

Surveillance Capitalism

Free services may be paid for with data used to predict, influence, and monetize behavior.

Reflect

Is a business model built around predicting and influencing behavior ethical? What would a privacy-respecting alternative look like?

Emerging Trends

What is coming next?

Emotion AI

Systems that claim to read emotions from face, voice, or typing patterns.

IoT Everything

Everyday devices collecting data and creating new privacy risks.

Biometric Creep

More places using fingerprints, face, voice, or other body signals for identification.

AI Surveillance Cities

Comprehensive systems that track movement, behavior, and public activity.

Rights and Research

Your rights, such as they are

Depending on where you live, privacy rights may include the right to access data, request deletion, or know how data is used. Rights vary widely by location and context.

Research Task

What data privacy laws exist where you live? What rights do students, families, and teachers actually have?

Teacher Education Bridge

For future teachers, this research should include district policy, approved tools, student data protections, and rules for using student work or identifiable information in AI systems.

Bottom Line

Understanding surveillance gives you power

AI-powered data collection is not going away. But understanding how it works can support better choices, better classroom guidance, and better advocacy.

Key Takeaways

  • Your data is constantly being collected, connected, and analyzed by AI systems.
  • This creates consequences for discrimination, manipulation, and loss of autonomy.
  • “Nothing to hide” is not a strong argument because privacy is about control, dignity, and freedom.
  • You cannot opt out completely, but you can make choices to protect yourself and your students.
  • This is both a personal and societal issue that needs bigger solutions.

Final question: What does it mean to be free in a world where AI systems may know more about you than you know about yourself?
