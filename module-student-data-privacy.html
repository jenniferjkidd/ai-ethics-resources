Module

Student Data, Privacy, and Surveillance

Help preservice teachers recognize that AI tools often depend on data collection, inference, tracking, and platform relationships that may affect students' privacy, autonomy, agency, and dignity.

Start moduleOpen privacy audit

Learning Focus

  • Student data is broader than names and grades
  • Personalization can become surveillance
  • Privacy is a professional responsibility

Opening

AI tools can feel private. Often, they are not.

Many AI systems are built around data: prompts, uploads, account details, usage patterns, student work, voice, images, clicks, locations, and behavioral traces. For teachers, student data is not just information. It can reveal identity, ability, disability, language background, family circumstances, academic struggle, behavior patterns, emotional disclosures, and future opportunities.

Learning Goals

Preservice teachers will be able to...

01

Identify data

Name forms of student data AI tools may collect.

02

Question necessity

Distinguish helpful personalization from unnecessary surveillance.

03

Assess consent

04

Recommend safeguards

Suggest privacy-preserving alternatives and boundaries.

Core Concepts

What should teachers notice?

Student data is bigger than names and grades

It may include writing, chat messages, voice, images, learning analytics, login times, behavior records, disability-related information, family details, and teacher notes.

Personalization can become surveillance

Tools may promise personalized help while collecting detailed patterns about what students know, how they work, when they struggle, and how they respond to feedback.

Privacy is not only an individual responsibility

Students can learn privacy habits, but teachers, schools, districts, vendors, and policymakers shape what students are asked to give up.

Use must match the learning purpose

The ethical question is whether the level of data collection is justified by the educational purpose.

Activity

Data Trail Mapping

Ask students to choose an AI-powered educational tool or platform. Then map the possible data trail.

  1. What does the student type, upload, say, click, or submit?
  2. What account or identity information is required?
  3. What learning or behavior patterns might the tool infer?
  4. Who might access the information?
  5. How long might it be stored?
  6. Could it be used to train or improve the system?
  7. What information should never be entered?

Discussion Scenario

A teacher wants students to use an AI feedback tool to revise essays before peer review. Students paste drafts into the tool and receive suggestions about structure, clarity, grammar, and tone.

Teacher Education Frame

From personal privacy to professional responsibility

Preservice teachers often think of AI privacy as a personal choice. This module reframes privacy as part of professional care. Teachers need to know what they can and cannot paste into AI tools, especially when working with student identities, student work, assessment data, family information, behavior records, disability-related information, or sensitive disclosures.
