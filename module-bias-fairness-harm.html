Activity-Support Module

Bias, Fairness, and Harm in AI

Bias in AI is not simply a technical glitch. It can enter through data, model design, categories, deployment contexts, and the unequal systems AI tools are built inside.

Start module Open pathway

Use With

  • AI grading tools
  • Predictive student-risk systems
  • Image generation and representation
  • Assessment, placement, and intervention decisions

Purpose

Help preservice teachers notice how bias becomes educational harm.

AI systems often appear objective because they use data and algorithms. But data reflects social patterns, institutional decisions, missing voices, and historical inequities. In education, biased AI can affect who is seen as capable, who is flagged as at risk, who receives support, whose language is valued, and whose work is misunderstood.

Core Concept

AI is a reflection of us, but sometimes it is warped.

AI does not simply “discover” neutral truth. It learns from human-created data, human-designed categories, and human institutions.

Data Bias

Training data may be incomplete, unbalanced, mislabeled, stereotyped, or shaped by past inequity.

Education example: A writing tool trained on narrow language patterns may misread multilingual students' work.

Model Bias

The model may optimize for a target, proxy, or category that looks measurable but does not reflect what educators actually value.

Education example: A grading system may reward surface-level features more than reasoning or creativity.

Systemic Bias

Even a technically accurate system can reproduce unequal school patterns, expectations, or access.

Education example: A risk system trained on discipline records may reproduce unequal discipline practices.

Feedback Bias

AI outputs can shape future data. More monitoring can create more records, which then justify more monitoring.

Education example: Students flagged as “at risk” may receive closer surveillance, creating more data that confirms the label.

Two Harms

How bias hurts people.

The distinction helps preservice teachers move from “the system is biased” to a sharper analysis of what kind of harm is happening.

Representational Harm

AI reinforces stereotypes, erases identities, distorts cultures, or portrays groups unfairly.

Ask: Who is misrepresented, erased, reduced, or framed as abnormal?

Allocative Harm

AI affects who gets access to opportunities, resources, recognition, support, or freedom.

Ask: Who gets more or less because of the system's output?

Activity

Spot the bias and harm.

Give students one scenario at a time. Ask them to name the likely bias source, the harm type, and what a teacher or school should do before trusting the system.

AI Grading

An AI essay scorer gives lower scores to students who use dialect features, multilingual phrasing, or unconventional structures, even when their ideas are strong.

Prompt: Is this data bias, model bias, systemic bias, or more than one?

Predictive Risk

A school system flags students for intervention using grades, attendance, assignment completion, online activity, and behavior notes.

Prompt: What could make the label helpful, and what could make it harmful?

Generated Examples

A teacher asks an AI tool for examples of “successful scientists,” and the first outputs mostly reflect one race, gender, language background, or region.

Prompt: What representational harm might students absorb?

Discussion Move

Push students to name both the technical issue and the educational consequence. “The data is biased” is a starting point, not a complete analysis.

Teacher Education Frame

Bias analysis should lead to professional action.

Preservice teachers do not need to become machine-learning engineers, but they do need habits for questioning AI outputs before those outputs shape students' opportunities.

  1. What student data or student work is being judged?
  2. Who might be misread by this tool?
  3. What harm could happen if the output is wrong?
  4. Can students, families, or teachers challenge the output?
  5. What human review is required before acting?
  6. What would count as evidence that the tool is fair enough for this context?

Facilitator Note

For teacher-preparation courses, keep returning to classroom judgment: when should a teacher question, override, document, disclose, or refuse an AI-generated recommendation?
