Module

Bias, Fairness, and Harm in AI

Bias in AI is not simply a technical glitch. It can enter through data, design choices, labels, categories, deployment contexts, and institutional decision-making.

Start moduleOpen worksheet

Key Distinction

  • Representational harm
  • Allocative harm
  • Data, design, and deployment bias

Opening

Data is not neutral.

AI systems are often described as objective because they use data and algorithms. But data reflects human decisions, social patterns, historical inequities, missing voices, and institutional priorities. In education, bias can affect who is seen as capable, who is flagged as at risk, who receives opportunities, who is disciplined, whose language is valued, and whose work is misunderstood.

Data Bias

If groups are underrepresented, mislabeled, stereotyped, or over-policed in data, systems may reproduce those patterns.

Design Bias

Every tool embeds choices about success, categories, acceptable errors, priorities, and testing groups.

Deployment Bias

The same tool has different ethical stakes when used for brainstorming versus grading, intervention, placement, or discipline.

Representational and Allocative Harm

Systems can stereotype or erase people, and they can also affect access to resources, support, discipline, or recognition.

Activity

Bias Pathway Analysis

Give students an AI education scenario: essay scoring, behavior prediction, reading placement, AI-generated lesson examples, proctoring, monitoring, or translation for family communication.

  1. What data might the system use?
  2. What patterns might be missing or distorted?
  3. What assumptions might the tool make?
  4. Who might be misread?
  5. What harm could result?
  6. How could a teacher check or challenge the system?

Discussion Scenario

A school uses AI to identify students who may need extra academic support. The tool flags students based on grades, assignment completion, attendance, online activity, and behavior notes.

Teacher Education Frame

Automation can scale unfairness.

A biased human process can become a biased automated process. Automation may make bias harder to see, harder to challenge, and easier to scale. Teachers need habits for questioning AI outputs, documenting concerns, involving students and families, and preserving human judgment.
