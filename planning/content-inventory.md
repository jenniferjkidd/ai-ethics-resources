## 2.2 | Where Training Data Comes From - And Why It's Controversial

Source type: Canvas page HTML

Current source file: `/Users/Jennifer/.codex/attachments/b43e8cb2-f85c-49fa-b7c8-1107bb89977e/pasted-text.txt`

Audience in original course: undergraduate AI literacy students across majors

Potential audience in revised hub: preservice teachers, teacher educator faculty

Student-facing? Yes, with adaptation

Estimated time: 30-45 minutes as a full lesson segment; 10-15 minutes if used only as an ethics case/activity

## Summary

This page explains how large language models generate text by tokenizing input, examining relationships among tokens, predicting next tokens, and repeating that process. It then uses that technical foundation to introduce ethical and legal controversies about training data: consent, ownership, public vs. okay-to-use, fair use, pirated data, market impact, transparency, and personal data.

## Core Learning Goals

- Explain that LLMs generate responses by predicting likely next tokens based on patterns learned from training data.
- Identify common sources of training data, including web snapshots, reference sites, books/articles, code, forums, and curated datasets.
- Explain why training-data collection creates ethical and legal controversy.
- Distinguish between "publicly available" and "ethically or legally okay to use."
- Consider whether personal online content, creative work, or chatbot conversations should be available for AI training.

## AI Ethics Topics

- consent and ownership;
- copyright and fair use;
- creator compensation and market impact;
- transparency of training datasets;
- privacy and personal data;
- data provenance;
- bias and representativeness;
- identity, likeness, and deepfakes;
- public availability versus ethical permission.

## UNESCO Alignment

Primary dimension: Ethics of AI

Supporting dimensions:

- AI foundations and applications, because learners need a basic model of how LLMs work to understand why training data matters.
- Human-centred mindset, because the page asks learners to consider whose data, work, identity, and agency are affected.

Progression level:

- Acquire, with possible Deepen extensions.

Relevant UNESCO Ethics of AI competencies:

- Ethical principles: learners examine controversies in AI use, including human agency, privacy, equity, inclusion, cultural relevance, and sustainability.
- Safe and responsible use: with revision, the activity could connect to data privacy, copyright, disclosure, and responsible classroom use.

## Education Use Cases

This page can support teacher-preparation conversations about:

- whether teachers or students should use AI tools trained on contested data;
- how to talk with K-12 students about where AI outputs come from;
- whether student work should be uploaded into AI platforms;
- privacy and consent when using student data or examples;
- copyright and attribution when teachers use AI-generated lesson materials;
- the ethics of asking students to use commercial AI systems.

## Reusable Teaching Pieces

### Strong keepers

- Big idea explaining that LLMs use human-created datasets and that this raises questions about what data is used and whether use is acceptable.
- Step-by-step LLM explanation: tokens, relationships, next-token prediction, repeated generation.
- Mini-demo: "What comes next?" as a quick embodied model of next-token prediction.
- Key idea: models predict from training-data patterns rather than remembering facts like humans.
- Clarification that LLMs generate responses rather than simply searching Google or copy-pasting answers.
- Training data source list: web snapshots, Wikipedia/reference sites, books/articles, code, forums/Q&A, curated collections.
- Transparency note: companies often do not disclose exact training data.
- Personal reflection prompt: Would you be okay with your online posts or class work being used to train AI?
- "Think it Through" activity: okay, not okay, or it depends for public review, public Instagram photo, published poem, and chatbot conversations.
- Final takeaway: legality and ethics of data use are evolving.

### Needs adaptation for teacher education

- Add explicit preservice-teacher framing: "How would you explain this to students?" and "What would you allow students to upload?"
- Add classroom cases involving student writing, teacher-created materials, IEP/504-related information, family communication, or field-placement artifacts.
- Add a facilitator note distinguishing legal uncertainty from ethical decision-making.
- Connect the copyright discussion to teacher-created lesson plans, student work samples, and AI-generated classroom materials.
- Include a tool-evaluation or policy question: "What should a teacher check before using a chatbot with students?"

## 2.5 | The Humans Behind the Curtain

## Summary

This page challenges the idea that AI is fully automated or objective by showing the human roles involved in data collection, labeling, moderation, rating, reinforcement learning from human feedback, system design, and deployment. It highlights invisible labor, outsourced data work, emotional harms to content moderators, the value judgments embedded in rating and design decisions, and the role of designers in defining AI purposes, data sources, safety rules, and acceptable uses.

## Core Learning Goals

- Describe human roles that shape AI systems before, during, and after training.
- Explain how labelers, moderators, raters, and designers influence what AI systems can and cannot do.
- Recognize that AI systems reflect human decisions, labor conditions, institutional values, and trade-offs.
- Question claims that AI systems are neutral, objective, or fully automated.
- Consider ethical responsibilities toward workers whose labor supports AI systems.

## AI Ethics Topics

- invisible labor;
- data labeling and annotation;
- content moderation and worker trauma;
- outsourcing and global labor inequities;
- reinforcement learning from human feedback;
- human judgment and AI "safety";

- accountability for AI behavior;

## Summary

## Core Learning Goals

- Explain the difference between representational harm and allocative harm.
- Recognize that AI can reflect, amplify, or automate existing social inequalities.
- Analyze real-world AI examples to identify bias type and harm type.
- Explain why quick technical fixes may not solve bias without context-aware design.

## Current Canvas Focus

This page serves as a capstone action/reflection activity. After learning about AI ethics, environmental costs, regulation, safety, dependency, and hidden harms, learners develop a personal AI use policy based on their values, boundaries, gray areas, and sense of individual versus collective responsibility.

## Strong Reusable Ideas

- Opening question: after learning about AI's benefits and harms, what role should AI play in your life?
- Learning targets: develop and justify a personal AI use policy; distinguish individual choices from collective/policy action.
- “AI vegans” concept as an analogy for intentional ethical abstention or selective use.
- Reasons for opting out or limiting use: ethical sourcing, environmental impact, personal wellness, skill preservation.
- AI engagement spectrum: maximalist, pragmatic, selective, minimalist, AI vegan.
- Poll asking learners where they fall on the engagement spectrum.
- “Your power and your limits” section distinguishing personal use choices, consumer choices, advocacy, and systemic decisions outside individual control.
- Paradox that people interact with AI even when they do not directly choose to use AI tools.
- Personal AI framework with three questions: values, red lines, gray zones.
- Civic reframe: not just user/consumer, but voter, worker, community member, creator/professional.
- Reflection assignment: create a personal AI use policy that includes spectrum position, values, red lines, gray zones, and plan for staying informed.
- Key takeaway that there is no single correct level of AI engagement, only informed versus uninformed choices.

## Teacher Education Relevance

Very high. This is a strong capstone resource because teacher educators can adapt it from a personal AI policy into a professional AI stance for preservice teachers. It asks future teachers to articulate how they will use AI responsibly for their own work, how they will talk with students about AI, and where they need classroom or institutional policies.

Useful for preservice teachers learning to:

- Develop a thoughtful professional stance toward AI use.
- Distinguish personal AI use from professional AI use with students.
- Set boundaries around student data, grading, feedback, lesson planning, communication, creative work, accessibility, and emotional support.
- Identify when individual choices are enough and when school/district policy or collective action is needed.
- Reflect on AI use as teachers, learners, workers, citizens, and community members.
- Communicate AI expectations transparently to students and families.
- Revisit and revise AI commitments as tools, policies, and teaching contexts change.
