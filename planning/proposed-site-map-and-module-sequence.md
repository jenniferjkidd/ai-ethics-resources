# Proposed Site Map and Module Sequence

Working purpose: turn the reviewed Canvas materials into a public-facing AI ethics resource hub for teacher educator faculty and preservice teachers.

This is a synthesis document. It does not replace the full content inventory; it turns that inventory into a proposed website structure, first-release sequence, and set of reusable tools.

## Guiding Idea

The hub should help faculty and preservice teachers move through four kinds of ethical work:

1. Understand what AI systems are made from and who is affected.
2. Analyze harms, benefits, trade-offs, and decision-making power.
3. Apply ethical judgment to education-specific tools and scenarios.
4. Make professional commitments for responsible classroom practice.

The site should not feel like a long course shell copied out of Canvas. It should feel like a practical, navigable resource library where faculty can quickly find a module, case, checklist, or activity.

### Tools and Evaluation Guides

Purpose: give faculty and preservice teachers practical decision aids.

Build these as reusable pages or downloadable worksheets:

- **AI Ethics Review Worksheet**
  - Based on 5.2.
  - Questions: Who benefits? Who risks harm? Who pays the cost? Who gets to decide?
- **AI Tool Privacy Audit**
  - Based on 4.5 and 4.4.
  - Questions: What data is collected? Is it used for training? Who can access it? What should never be uploaded?
- **AI Tool Safety Checklist**
  - Based on 5.4, 4.6, and 3.4.
  - Questions: What guardrails exist? What happens in crisis? Is there human oversight? Is the tool appropriate for minors?
- **Learning vs. Producing Decision Guide**
  - Based on 4.7.
  - Questions: What cognitive work must students do themselves? What can AI support without undermining the learning goal?
- **Professional AI Stance Template**
  - Based on 5.5.
  - Sections: AI for my work, AI with students, AI I will not use with students, uses requiring approval, disclosure commitments, revision plan.
- **School AI Policy Map**
  - Based on 5.3 and 5.2.
  - Levels: classroom, course, school, district, state, federal, platform terms, professional ethics.

### Cases and Dilemmas

Purpose: provide discussion-ready scenarios where there is no single easy answer.

Candidate cases:

- AI tutor adoption: personalization vs. privacy and human support.
- AI grading or feedback tool: efficiency vs. fairness, validity, and transparency.
- Student chatbot/companion: support vs. dependency, privacy, and referral obligations.
- Deepfake incident at school: verification, reporting, dignity, and safety.
- Data center proposal: jobs and innovation vs. water, energy, and community impact.
- Predictive student-risk system: early support vs. labeling, bias, and surveillance.
- Facial recognition on campus or in schools: safety claims vs. privacy and unequal harm.
- AI-generated lesson materials: teacher efficiency vs. copyright, bias, and quality.

### Faculty PD Activities

Purpose: support Team AI sessions and other professional development.

Suggested activities:

- Run the four-question ethics framework on a tool faculty are considering.
- Have faculty classify assignments as learn it, practice it, produce it, or automate it.
- Compare two AI policies and identify missing voices.
- Build a shared list of student-data red lines.
- Draft discipline-specific AI use scenarios for preservice teachers.
- Use the professional AI stance template personally, then adapt it for preservice teachers.

## Suggested Module Sequence for a Faculty PD Session

If the resource hub is used to support a Team AI ethics session, a strong session arc would be:

1. **Opening Question**
   - What do preservice teachers need to know, understand, and be able to do about AI ethics?

2. **Framework**
   - Introduce the four-question framework from 5.2.
   - Apply it quickly to an AI tutor, grading tool, or student chatbot.

3. **Education-Specific Ethical Risks**
   - Student data and privacy.
   - Bias and fairness.
   - Learning vs. producing.
   - Student wellbeing and AI companions if time allows.

4. **Faculty Application**
   - Faculty choose one course activity or assignment.
   - They identify the AI ethics issue most relevant to that course.
   - They select one resource, checklist, or case they could use.

5. **Professional Commitment**
   - Faculty draft one AI-use principle for preservice teachers in their course.

## Design Notes for the Website

The site should feel like a working educator resource, not a marketing page.

Recommended homepage layout:

- concise title and purpose;
- quick start paths:
  - "I need a class activity";
  - "I need a tool checklist";
  - "I need a case discussion";
  - "I am planning a faculty PD session";
- featured tools;
- featured modules;
- topic filters.

Resource card fields:

- title;
- short teacher-facing description;
- time needed;
- best audience;
- activity type;
- UNESCO alignment;
- status: ready, needs adaptation, needs source check.

## Working Decisions

- Do not publish everything at once.
- Prioritize teacher-facing usability over preserving Canvas sequence.
- Treat some pages as source material for reusable tools rather than as standalone modules.
- Keep current/legal/product-specific claims out of the first release unless they have been verified.
- Recreate interactive elements locally where possible, especially if third-party tools collect data or require login.
