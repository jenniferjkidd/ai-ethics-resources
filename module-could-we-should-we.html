Ready-to-Teach Module

Could We, Should We?

An ethical decision-making module that helps preservice teachers evaluate AI tools by asking who benefits, who risks harm, who pays the cost, and who gets a voice before systems are adopted.

Start the module Open worksheet

Module Snapshot

  • Suggested time: 45 to 75 minutes
  • Audience: preservice teachers
  • Format: discussion, small-group case analysis, decision memo
  • Primary focus: Ethics of AI

Faculty Overview

Use the framework before the tool feels inevitable.

This module gives preservice teachers a reusable way to evaluate AI decisions in classrooms, schools, and teacher professional practice. It is designed for moments when an AI tool seems useful, efficient, or innovative, but the ethical tradeoffs are not yet visible.

Works Well In

  • Foundations of education
  • Educational technology
  • Methods courses
  • Assessment courses
  • Field-experience seminars

Learning Goals

Preservice teachers will be able to...

01

Identify stakeholders

Name students, families, teachers, school leaders, vendors, and communities affected by an AI decision.

02

Analyze tradeoffs

Distinguish convenience, efficiency, access, privacy, bias, labor, cost, and learning impacts.

03

Recommend safeguards

Propose conditions such as transparency, opt-out options, human oversight, data minimization, and ongoing review.

04

Justify a decision

Explain whether an AI use should proceed, pause, change, or be rejected in a specific educational context.

Student-Facing Activity

Evaluate an AI decision using four questions.

This activity can be run with one shared class case or with small groups evaluating different cases.

5 min

Opening Question

Think of an AI tool that could be used in a classroom or school. What makes it appealing? What gives you pause?

10 min

Introduce the Framework

Explain that ethical AI decisions require more than asking whether a tool works. Students use the four questions to surface benefits, harms, costs, and decision-making power.

20 min

Small-Group Case Analysis

Groups choose one case below and complete the AI Ethics Review Worksheet. They should name both likely benefits and conditions that would need to be met before use.

15 min

Decision Memo

Each group makes one recommendation: use as proposed, use with safeguards, pause for more information, or do not use in this context.

10 min

Debrief

Compare how different groups weighed benefits, risks, costs, and voice. Emphasize that ethical judgment depends on context and affected people.

Case Options

Choose one scenario for analysis.

AI Tutor Adoption

A school is considering an AI tutor that gives personalized practice and feedback. The vendor says it can help students who need extra support, but it requires student accounts and collects learning data.

AI Writing Feedback

A teacher wants students to paste drafts into an AI tool for feedback before peer review. Some students may benefit from immediate support, but the tool may store student writing and shape revision choices.

Predictive Student-Risk System

A district wants to identify students who may need intervention by using attendance, grades, behavior records, and platform activity. The goal is early support, but students may be labeled or monitored unevenly.

Framework

The four questions

Students should answer these questions with evidence from the case, not only personal opinion.

Who benefits?

What becomes easier, more accessible, more efficient, or more effective? Who benefits most directly?

Who risks harm?

Who might be misjudged, excluded, surveilled, pressured, or made dependent? What happens if the system is wrong?

Who pays the cost?

What data, attention, money, labor, trust, energy, or opportunity is required? Who carries that cost?

Who gets to decide?

Were students, families, teachers, and affected communities consulted? Is there transparency, appeal, or opt-out?

Facilitator Notes

Help students stay with the tension.

Do not let the discussion become only pro-AI or anti-AI.

Ask students to name real benefits and real risks. The goal is better judgment, not a simple yes-or-no position.

Keep power and voice visible.

Push students to ask whether the people most affected by the AI decision had meaningful input before adoption.

Make safeguards concrete.

“Use with caution” is too vague. Ask for specific conditions: data limits, human review, disclosure, opt-out, accessibility checks, and evaluation plans.

Connect back to teaching identity.

Invite students to name what professional responsibility requires when a tool is useful but also risky.

Extension Options

Adapt the module for your course.

  • Ask students to apply the worksheet to an AI tool they have personally used.
  • Assign groups different stakeholder roles: student, family member, teacher, administrator, counselor, vendor, or community advocate.
  • Have students draft conditions for approval before seeing another group’s recommendation.
  • Pair this module with the AI Tool Privacy Audit for a deeper focus on student data.
  • Ask students to revise an assignment or classroom policy based on their recommendation.
