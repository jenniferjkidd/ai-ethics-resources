Could We, Should We?

Just because we can build an AI system does not mean we should. This lesson helps learners ask: Who benefits, who risks harm, who pays the costs, and who gets to decide?

Start lesson Open worksheet

Learning Target

I can use an ethical framework to evaluate who benefits, who is harmed, and who decides when AI systems are developed or deployed.

Teacher Education Frame

Why this matters for preservice teachers

Teachers will encounter AI as classroom tools, tutoring systems, grading supports, student-facing chatbots, surveillance systems, policy decisions, and district purchases. This lesson gives preservice teachers a practical way to slow down and evaluate AI decisions before they become normal classroom practice.

Use This Lesson As

A class reading, small-group case discussion, ethics-board activity, or launch point for evaluating an AI tool students may use in field placements or future classrooms.

The Framework

The key question framework

When evaluating any AI application, ask these four essential questions.

Who benefits?

Identifies the primary winners and intended positive impacts.

Who risks harm?

Reveals potential negative consequences and vulnerable populations.

Who pays the costs?

Examines financial, social, environmental, privacy, and relational burdens.

Who gets to decide?

Questions power dynamics and representation in decision-making.

Case Study 1

Facial recognition technology

Apply the framework to one of today’s most controversial AI applications: facial recognition systems.

Who benefits?
  • Law enforcement agencies: faster identification of suspects and reduced investigation time.
  • Security companies: new revenue streams from selling and maintaining systems.
  • Some community members: potential increased sense of safety.
  • Local government: political wins from appearing “tough on crime.”

The promised benefit: safer streets and quicker resolution of crimes.

Who risks harm?
  • People of color: greater risk of misidentification and disproportionate policing.
  • Women: higher misidentification concerns in many facial recognition systems.
  • Political activists: potential surveillance and chilling effects on speech and assembly.
  • Everyone: loss of anonymity in public spaces.
  • Innocent people: risk of wrongful arrest or investigation after a false match.
Who pays the costs?

Financial Costs

  • Taxpayers: system installation, maintenance, and legal settlements.
  • Falsely accused individuals: legal fees, lost wages, and psychological trauma.

Social Costs

  • Marginalized communities: increased distrust of law enforcement.
  • Society: erosion of privacy rights and normalization of surveillance.
  • Democracy: chilling effects on peaceful protest and political organizing.
Who gets to decide?
  • City councils and police departments may make decisions with limited public input.
  • Communities most affected rarely have a meaningful seat at the table.
  • Technology companies influence decisions through lobbying and contracts.
  • Residents may learn about systems only after deployment.

The problem: those making the decision are often not the same people bearing the highest risks.

The ethical verdict

Using the framework, we see a troubling pattern: benefits are concentrated, risks are distributed unequally, costs are socialized, and decision-making is exclusionary.

Key takeaway: when benefits accrue to the powerful and risks fall on the vulnerable, we need to pump the brakes.

Case Study 2

AI Tutor: You are on the design team

Imagine a school or university wants to implement an AI tutoring system that personalizes learning and provides 24/7 homework help. Theoretically, it could track everything: assignment completion, time spent on readings, quiz performance, typing speed, and patterns. But should it?

Your design challenge

You have been invited to help design the AI tutor. Your choices involve tradeoffs around personalization, data collection, privacy, equity, funding, and governance.

  1. Start the simulation or use the reflection prompts below as a non-login alternative.
  2. Track the choices you make and the tradeoffs they create.
  3. Apply the four-question framework to your design.
Start simulation
Who benefits?

In the system you designed, who came out ahead? Did you think equally about all the groups who could benefit?

  • Students get personalized support anytime.
  • Struggling students may receive extra help earlier.
  • The institution may serve more students with fewer human tutors.
  • Instructors may receive data about where students struggle.
  • The technology company earns licensing fees.
  • Students without transportation may access tutoring from home.
Who risks harm?
  • All students risk privacy violations if detailed behavioral data is breached, sold, or misused.
  • Neurodivergent students may be flagged as “unusual learners.”
  • Students with disabilities might receive weaker accommodations than they would from human support.
  • Students in crisis could interact with AI instead of a human who might recognize distress.
  • Lower-income students may be tracked more heavily if they rely on the system more often.
  • Human tutors may lose work or professional roles.
Who pays the costs?
  • Students may pay through tuition, fees, or loss of privacy.
  • Student data may become a commodity.
  • Human tutors may pay with lost employment and expertise.
  • Academic relationships may suffer if human mentorship is replaced.
  • The institution must pay for data storage, security, and oversight.
Who gets to decide?
  • Administrators often make purchasing decisions.
  • Technology companies design the system and control the algorithm.
  • Students are primary users but rarely have a vote.
  • Faculty may or may not be consulted about pedagogical implications.
  • Families may not be informed about data practices.

The verdict

This case is more nuanced than facial recognition. The AI tutoring system offers genuine benefits, especially for access and support. However, the power imbalance is striking: students may have the most to lose and the least say in whether or how the system is deployed.

Scale Shift

Personal vs. institutional ethics

Ethical stakes escalate when AI moves from a personal tool to a system that affects entire populations without their consent.

Personal Level

“Should I use AI to help with this assignment?”

  • Benefit: better understanding or faster completion.
  • Risk: academic dishonesty or skill atrophy.
  • Cost: integrity or genuine learning.
  • Who decides: you, within institutional policies.

Institutional Level

“Should we deploy this AI system?”

  • Benefit: efficiency, innovation, competitive advantage.
  • Risk: discrimination, privacy violations, or loss of trust.
  • Cost: financial, reputational, and social.
  • Who decides: often not the people most affected.

Reflection Activity

You are on the ethics board

Your university or school system is reviewing these AI proposals. Pick one, use the four-question framework, and make a recommendation.

Proposal A: Mental Health Chatbot

An AI chatbot provides 24/7 mental health support to students. It can screen for depression and anxiety, offer coping strategies, and escalate serious cases to human counselors.

Proposal B: Grade Prediction System

An AI analyzes student data to predict which students will struggle academically, triggering early interventions.

For your proposal, analyze:

  1. Who benefits? Be specific.
  2. Who risks harm? Think beyond the obvious.
  3. Who pays the costs? Consider hidden costs.
  4. Who gets to decide? Who should decide?
  5. Your verdict: approve, reject, or approve with conditions? What safeguards would you require?

Key Takeaway

The most powerful question is not “Can we build this?”

It is: Should we build this, and for whom?

When benefits flow to those with power while risks fall on vulnerable populations, and when affected people have no voice in the decision, innovation may be crossing ethical lines.

Responsible AI development means asking these hard questions before deployment, not after the damage is done.
