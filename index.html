Part 1

The Energy Appetite of AI

Every AI interaction depends on electricity: during model training, during deployment, and during each user request. Simple text queries may be relatively small per use, but heavier tasks and enormous usage volume change the ethical picture.

Energy Use Estimates
Activity Estimated energy use Everyday comparison Relative scale
Typical Google search ~0.2 to 0.3 Wh Power a small LED bulb for about 18 minutes Baseline
ChatGPT, GPT-3-era ~2 to 4 Wh Charge a phone about one-fifth to one-third 10 to 20x a Google search
ChatGPT, GPT-4 or GPT-4o-style text query ~0.3 to 0.5 Wh Keep a small LED bulb lit for 18 to 30 minutes Roughly similar to, or slightly higher than, a search
Complex multimodal query ~1 to 2 Wh Power a laptop for 2 to 4 minutes 5 to 10x a Google search
AI image generation ~10 to 50 Wh Fully charge 1 to 2 smartphones 50 to 200x a Google search
Bottom line: Per-query efficiency is improving, but total energy use keeps rising as AI use expands and tasks become more computationally demanding.
Teaching move

Ask students to compare per-use impact with system-wide impact. A small number multiplied billions of times becomes an ethical issue.

Part 2

Water, Cooling, and Local Trade-offs

Data centers generate heat, and many facilities depend on water-based cooling. The ethical question is not only how much water is used, but where it is used and who else needs it.

  • Some estimates place Google data center water use near 6 billion gallons of fresh water in 2023.
  • Training GPT-3 has been estimated to directly consume about 700,000 liters of water for cooling, with more indirect water use tied to energy production.
  • Some reports estimate an average data center can use about 300,000 gallons of water per day.
  • Chip manufacturing can require large amounts of ultra-pure water.

Discussion scenario

Your city is experiencing a drought. A tech company wants to build an AI data center that will use 500 million gallons of water per year but create 200 jobs. Would you support it? Why or why not?

Part 3

E-Waste and the Hardware Problem

AI is not only software. It depends on servers, GPUs, cooling systems, network infrastructure, and device upgrades. Those material needs connect AI to mining, manufacturing, and e-waste.

AI training can require thousands of GPUs running continuously. These systems can become obsolete quickly and require minerals such as lithium, cobalt, tantalum, silicon, and rare earth elements.

Mineral extraction can displace communities, pollute water, destroy habitats, and rely on exploitative labor practices. The physical footprint of AI starts far from the screen.

Part 4

Carbon Emissions and Climate Impact

Energy demand becomes a climate issue when the electricity mix still includes fossil fuels. Even when companies buy renewable energy, growing demand can outpace new clean power.

24/7

Data centers run continuously, not only when learners use a tool.

2-3 years

High-demand hardware may become outdated quickly.

Billions

Small per-query costs grow when scaled across global usage.

Systems thinking

From Rock to Screen

Your AI conversation began before you typed a question. It moved through extraction, processing, manufacturing, shipping, data centers, interfaces, and disposal.

1Mining

Cobalt, lithium, silicon, and rare earth elements enter the chain.

2Processing

Raw materials are refined, often with toxic waste and local impacts.

3Manufacturing

Chips, servers, and devices require water, energy, and labor.

4Shipping

Components move through global logistics networks.

5Data centers

Facilities use electricity, cooling, land, and infrastructure.

6Your screen

Users experience the tool while much of the impact remains hidden.

7E-waste

Obsolete hardware creates disposal and toxic exposure problems.

Part 7

So Should We Stop Using AI?

The productive ethical stance is not panic or blind optimism. AI may help optimize energy grids, model climate systems, improve materials, or reduce waste. The question is whether a particular use is worth its costs and who gets to decide.

Who benefits from this AI system?
Who bears the environmental or social cost?
Is there a less resource-intensive way to do this?
Is this application worth its hidden price tag?
