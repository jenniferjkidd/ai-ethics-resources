AI Ethics Review Worksheet

Use this worksheet to evaluate an AI tool, classroom activity, school system, or institutional decision before using it with students.

Start worksheet

Core Question

Could we use AI here? And more importantly, should we?

  • Who benefits?
  • Who risks harm?
  • Who pays the cost?
  • Who gets to decide?

Purpose

Move from reaction to judgment.

This tool helps preservice teachers slow down and examine an AI use case from multiple ethical angles before deciding whether, when, and how it belongs in an educational setting.

For Students Using This Page

Step 1

Describe the AI use case.

Start with the specific situation. Ethical judgment gets fuzzy when the example is too broad.

Step 2

Ask the four ethics questions.

Use these questions to surface tradeoffs that may be hidden by convenience, novelty, or pressure to adopt a tool quickly.

Who benefits?

Look for instructional, financial, institutional, and personal benefits.

  • What does this make easier, faster, more accessible, or more effective?
  • Who benefits most directly?
  • Who might be left out of the benefit?

Who risks harm?

Consider harms that may be immediate, delayed, visible, or unevenly distributed.

  • Could this create bias, exclusion, surveillance, dependency, or inaccurate judgments?
  • Which students or communities are most vulnerable to harm?
  • What would happen if the system is wrong?

Who pays the cost?

Costs may include money, time, data, attention, labor, energy, water, and opportunity.

  • What data, labor, or resources does the tool depend on?
  • What might teachers or students give up by using it?
  • Are environmental or community costs part of the decision?

Who gets to decide?

Ethical AI use depends on voice, consent, transparency, and accountability.

  • Who chose this tool or practice?
  • Do students and families know when AI is being used?
  • Can people ask questions, opt out, appeal, or get human review?

Step 3

Connect the ethics to teaching and learning.

For education, the question is not only whether the tool works. It is whether the tool supports the kind of learning, care, and professional judgment the situation requires.

Step 4

Make a provisional decision.

A responsible decision may change when the context, evidence, policy, or available alternatives change. The goal is to make your reasoning visible.

Based on your review, what would you recommend?

Discussion Prompts

Extend the conversation.

Compare Decisions

Where did your group agree or disagree? What values shaped the disagreement?

Change the Context

Would your decision change for a different grade level, subject area, school setting, or student population?

Name a Professional Commitment

What principle would guide your future classroom decisions about AI?
