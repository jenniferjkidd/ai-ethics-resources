Decision Guide

Learning vs. Producing

Help preservice teachers decide when AI supports learning and when it bypasses the cognitive work students need to practice.

Start guide Open stance template

Core Question

What are students learning, and what role should AI play in that learning?

Opening

A finished product is not always the learning goal.

AI can help people produce summaries, outlines, lesson ideas, emails, explanations, images, and polished writing. But in school, the product is often evidence of a deeper process: noticing, reasoning, drafting, revising, explaining, connecting, and making judgments.

Cognitive Offloading

The question is not whether tools help. It is what they help us stop practicing.

Writing offloads memory. Calculators offload computation. GPS offloads navigation. Generative AI can offload writing, reasoning, synthesis, planning, feedback, and decision-making. That is powerful, but it means teachers need to name which cognitive work students still need to do themselves.

Learning Practice

The task builds skill, fluency, judgment, confidence, or metacognition. AI risk: the tool does the thinking students need to practice.

Production Support

The task produces a useful artifact. AI may help with drafting, formatting, translation, brainstorming, or editing when those are not the learning target.

Demonstration of Learning

The task is evidence of what a student knows or can do. AI risk: the artifact no longer shows the student's understanding.

Access Support

The task includes unnecessary barriers. AI may support accessibility, organization, comprehension, or feedback while preserving the learning goal.

Green, Yellow, Red

Sort AI use by its relationship to the learning goal.

Green Light: Support

AI helps with work that is not central to the learning goal, or it removes barriers while students still do the target thinking.

  • Generate practice questions after reading
  • Proofread grammar after drafting
  • Translate directions for initial access
  • Format citations after sources are chosen

Yellow Light: Use With Conditions

AI can support the task, but students need boundaries, process evidence, disclosure, or explanation.

  • Brainstorm lesson ideas before selecting one
  • Get feedback on a draft students wrote
  • Ask for alternative explanations of a concept
  • Compare AI suggestions to course criteria

Red Light: Do Not Outsource

AI would replace the practice or evidence the assignment is meant to develop.

  • Write the field reflection students are learning to compose
  • Complete a reading response before reading
  • Generate a lesson rationale students must defend
  • Analyze student work when the goal is teacher judgment

Decision Guide

Ask before assigning or allowing AI use.

  1. What is the learning goal?
  2. What cognitive work matters most?
  3. What part of the task is practice?
  4. What part is production, formatting, or access support?
  5. Where could AI help without replacing the target skill?
  6. Where would AI make the evidence of learning unreliable?
  7. What should students disclose or explain?
  8. How will I assess process, judgment, or understanding?

Facilitator Move

Ask students to complete the sentence: “The part students must do themselves is ___ because the learning goal is ___.”

Teacher Prep Examples

Apply the distinction to common course tasks.

Field reflection

Risk: AI can produce a polished reflection without the student practicing observation, interpretation, or professional noticing.

Better design: Students use field notes to identify one moment, explain what they noticed, connect it to course ideas, and include a short AI-use note if they used AI for proofreading only.

Lesson planning

Risk: AI can generate an attractive activity that is not aligned to learning goals, students, context, or assessment.

Better design: Students may ask AI for options, but must justify which option fits the learning goal, what they changed, and how they would know students learned.

Reading response

Risk: AI summary can replace reading, confusion, annotation, and connection-making.

Better design: Students identify one quote, one claim, one question, and one classroom implication before using AI to compare or extend their thinking.

Family communication draft

Risk: AI may produce generic, insensitive, or privacy-risky messages.

Better design: Students draft the message purpose, audience, and sensitive details themselves, then use AI only to check tone or clarity with identifying details removed.

Student Disclosure

Require students to explain the role AI played.

Disclosure works best when it asks students to describe process, not just confess tool use.

AI Use Note Template

I used AI for: [brainstorming / feedback / editing / explanation / formatting / other].

I did not use AI for: [the part of the task I needed to practice or demonstrate].

I changed, rejected, or checked the AI output by: [specific action].

The final judgment or explanation is mine because: [brief rationale].

Example Redesign

Original: Write a one-page reflection about what you learned from your field observation.

Possible redesign: Use your field notes to identify one moment when a teacher supported student thinking. Discuss the moment with a partner. Then write a reflection explaining what you noticed, how you interpreted it, and what question it raises for your future teaching. You may use AI to proofread grammar after drafting, but not to generate the reflection. If you use AI, include an AI Use Note.
